What happens to your vote after you've voted

Description
Most of us will vote in person on election day, but some of us will cast our vote use other voting options in the lead up to the election. Watch as we explain how your vote is counted, no matter how you voted, and the processes involved after the polls close.

Understanding vote types

Vote type Description
Ordinary A vote cast on election day at a voting centre within the district the voter is enrolled to vote in.
Absent A vote cast on election day at a voting centre located outside of the district the voter is enrolled to vote in.
Early A vote cast prior to election day (includes mobile voting, telephone assisted voting, and any votes cast at an interstate voting centre).
Postal A vote cast by post.
Provisional A vote cast at a voting centre where the voter enrolled and voted on the spot.
Marked as voted A vote cast at a voting centre by someone who claims not to have voted, but who appears to have been marked off the roll. The vote will not be counted until we are satisfied that there has been no instance of multiple voting.

  • Saturday 26 November

    What gets counted on election night?

    • Counting starts at 6 pm on election day, after voting centres close.
    • All ordinary votes will be counted tonight.
    • We will also begin counting Lower House early votes and postal votes.

    Will there be a result tonight?

    First preference votes counted tonight may provide an indication of the election if a candidate receives more than 50% of the enrolled votes. However, around half of the district results will likely involve a preference distribution after a full recheck of all votes.

    This process takes some days, and a final official result (known as a declaration) will not be available for up to a week or more.
